SIMULATION IN MEDICAL EDUCATION.

Breathing / bleeding / defibrillatable humanoid simulators, objective structured clinical examinations (OSCEs), and dissolving the ugly complexity of medical software..

Click or Tap the Images above to view full PDFs

  • Employer: B-Line Medical, LLC. / Date: October 2014 - June 2015. / Category: Interaction Design, Information Architecture.

Pioneering UX and Design Thinking at an enterprise medical software company = the hard knock life. As a colleague put it, "complication you understand versus complication you don't understand." However - the mission was invaluable, the challenges met incredible rewards, and more bricks were removed from the wall that separates healthcare from meaningful innovation. After 10 years of Flash, it was time for a complete reboot in HTML5. From HIPAA Compliance and privacy concerns to strict curriculum and limited access to users, my main battle was data; where to find it and how to validate it. In UX speak, 'where is the why?'

For this, I relied upon the power of communication; department hopping, on-site research, internal training and even meetups focused on medical education. These manifested into an intuitive application for Google Glass, debuted to hundereds of current and potential customers at the International Meeting for Simulation in Healthcare (IMSH) - a first in the field. Why the Harry Potter personas, you ask? What better way to remember 9 personas who share an educational institution, yet nothing else while specializing in very specific fields? Using a theme with these personas and matching each of our actual user types with a well-known Hogwarts character not only helped me remember our users' struggles, but helped my colleagues remember as well. Also worth mentioning, they look pretty cool.
