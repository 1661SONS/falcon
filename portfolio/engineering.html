ENGINEERING.

EPA National Sustainable Design Expo for People, Prosperity and the Planet:
Low Temperature Combustion with Reduced PM and NOx Emissions, Achieved by n-Butanol in-Port Injected in an Omnivorous Diesel Engine

A biodiesel engine running on the oil of cottonseeds donated by local Georgia farmers

  • Client: US Environmental Protection Agency. / Date: August 2012 - May 2013. / Category: Green Design, Vroom.

Before my UX career was official, there was this; UXing engines in our Renewable Energy and Engines Lab. The objective of this project was to reduce the amount of soot and NOx gases produced during combustion, while operating on a sustainable fuel. Because NOx gases form at high temperatures, this was achieved by delaying ignition of the fuel mix in the combustion chamber, thus lowering the combustion's temperature. Also, since soot is made up of burnt fuel and matter particulate, lowering the combustion temperature reduced its production as well.

The soot and NOx was further choked out by introducing an Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR) system. This kept the unspent fuel inside of the system, reintroducing it to the combustion chamber. The recycling of the engine's emissions dramatically reduced the soot and NOx production, both by over 50%. Unfortunately, this 'choking' also resulted in horsepower loss. Phase II of the grant award is being used to fund the research, design, and installation of a supercharger to regain the lost power. In south Georgia, there is no short of agricultural waste. From cottonseed oil to chicken fat, my fellow engineers at GSU are busy formulating biodiesels with just the right energy density and viscosity donated by local farmers to run our engines, and further our research.
